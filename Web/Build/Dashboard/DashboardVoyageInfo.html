Voyage Overview Curves: Guidance of Technical Superintendents

Voyage Overview Curves provides graphic information on vessel’s progress obtained from the daily noon report. It shows the events that affect operational efficiency and tracks actual voyage execution. Default view is 7 days, but can be selected up to 60 days. Ship staff shall endeavour to operate the vessel in an efficient manner. These curves help in identifying deviations, where corrective measure may be required.

A. Speed, Trim & Displacement Analysis

  • Difference in SOG & LOG, indicates presence of current
  • If current is against, LOG is greater than SOG
  • If current is following, SOG is greater than LOG
  • Trim should not be negative (vessel down by head)

B. RPM, Load & Slip

  • If current is following, slip will be less or negative
  • In rough weather, slip will increase
  • When the wind and sea are from ahead, slip will increase
  • When if the vessel has a fouled bottom, slip will increase
  • After drydocking, hull cleaning, slip will decrease,
  • Reduce the RPM in rough weather, to prevent engine racing and overloading.
  • ME Load should not exceed ME Load Limit, to prevent engine overloading
  • If RPM is Zero, ME Load is Zero, SOG & LOG is Zero, engine stopped, vessel drifting

Propeller Slip as a weather index
Slip may be considered as the difference between the speed of the vessel and the speed of the engine. It is always expressed as a percentage.

Example:- During 24hrs of a period ship's propeller shaft was observed to turn 87 RPM. the pitch of the propeller was 3.8 m. the observed ships speed over ground was 10knts. Calculate the value of the propeller slip during this period. (A nautical mile equals 1852m.)

C. FOC Analysis

  • During rough weather, FOC Rate may exceed CP Rate, reduce the speed
  • Folded and rough hull will increase FOC rate.
  • After drydocking, hull cleaning, FOC rate will decrease

Optimised Ship Handling

(i) Optimum trim
Most ships are designed to carry a certain amount of cargo at a designated speed, consuming a certain amount of fuel under a specified trim condition. Trim optimisation has significant economic benefits in terms of fuel savings. It should be emphasised that even small trim changes can have a large impact on vessel performance.

The change in ship trim is achieved via cargo load planning and a ballasting plan when in port. Nowadays, loading computers provide additional functionality for the representation of trim, optimal trim and loading options to achieve the optimal trim. Trim linked to a loading computer helps the deck officers to achieve a better loading plan and optimal trim.

Based on what has been described in this section, the following may be stated:
  1. Trim could influence ship fuel consumption significantly, with the evidence showing up to 0.5 % to 3% of savings.
  2. Trim impact is the result of changes to ship hydrodynamics and thereby ship resistances.
  3. For every ship, there is an optimal trim that will give minimal ship resistances and maximise fuel efficiency.
  4. The optimum trim is a function of ship speed and draft.
  5. To achieve optimal trim, due consideration should be given to ship loading and its load planning.
  6. Ballast water, and to some extent bunker fuel, may be used to trim the vessel.
  7. Most loading computers on-board provide functionalities and trim tables to achieve the desirable ship trim.
  8. It is difficult to measure the actual saving levels due to trim while the ship is underway due to impact of other operational conditions (speed, draft, weather and sea impacts).

(ii) Optimum ballast
Ships normally carry ballast water to ensure ship’s stability and safety. Normally, ballast levels should be adjusted, taking the requirements into account to meet ship stability, steering aspects of the ship and optimum trim. This does not necessarily mean carrying lots of ballast water all the time. This unnecessarily increases the ship displacement that directly increases fuel consumption. Thus, there is an optimum ballast condition that needs to be achieved through good cargo planning as well as voyage planning.

(iii) Optimum use of rudder and autopilot
Autopilot is the use of an automatic system to control the rudder on the vessel. Use of autopilot can reduce the fuel consumption by smoothing out the large angle rudder movements used to hold a steady course. Efficient and adaptive autopilot operations allow small deviations to course-line, but will use fewer and smaller angle rudder movements to maintain the course-line. This decreases the rudder movement and consequently reduces fuel consumption.
